    MCQ 1
    Q1. Moderator is used in a nuclear reactor to:
    • A) Slow down fast neutrons
    • B) Speed up the neutrons
    • C) Both A and B
    • D) None of these
    In a nuclear fission reactor, fast neutrons released during fission are too energetic to efficiently trigger further fission in U-235. A moderator (commonly water, heavy water, or graphite) slows these fast neutrons down to thermal speeds through elastic collisions, increasing the chance they're captured and sustain the chain reaction.
    MCQ 2
    Q2. Resistivity of a material depends on:
    • A) Material
    • B) Temperature
    • C) Length
    • D) Both A and B
    Resistivity (ρ) is an intrinsic property — it depends on what the material is made of and its temperature (which affects electron mobility), but not on the conductor's geometry. Resistance (R = ρL/A) depends on length and area, but resistivity itself doesn't.
    MCQ 3
    Q3. The electron volt (eV) is a unit of:
    • A) Potential
    • B) Energy
    • C) Power
    • D) Dispersion
    One electron volt is the kinetic energy gained by an electron when accelerated through a potential difference of one volt. It's a unit of energy, used because SI joules are inconveniently large at atomic and nuclear scales.
    MCQ 4
    Q4. If a ball is dropped from a height, which quantity is conserved throughout the fall (ignoring air resistance)?
    • A) Kinetic energy
    • B) Potential energy
    • C) Total mechanical energy
    • D) None of these
    As the ball falls, potential energy converts into kinetic energy, so neither stays constant on its own. With no non-conservative forces like air resistance, the total mechanical energy (KE + PE) remains constant throughout.
    MCQ 5
    Q5. Which of the following is NOT an example of simple harmonic motion (SHM)?
    • A) Motion of a spring
    • B) Two trains moving away from each other
    • C) A plucked guitar string
    • D) Vibration of an elastic band
    SHM needs a restoring force proportional to displacement, producing periodic oscillation about equilibrium. A spring, plucked string, and elastic band all oscillate this way. Two trains moving apart is just straight-line motion with no restoring force — not periodic at all.
    MCQ 6
    Q6. Newton's third law of motion (every action has an equal and opposite reaction) is essentially a consequence of the law of conservation of:
    • A) Mass
    • B) Energy
    • C) Momentum
    • D) Charge
    When two bodies interact, the forces they exert on each other are equal and opposite so that the total momentum of the isolated system stays unchanged — this is conservation of momentum expressed through Newton's third law.
    MCQ 7
    Q7. A laser produces light that is:
    • A) Monochromatic
    • B) Coherent
    • C) Highly directional
    • D) All of these
    Laser light is monochromatic (single wavelength), coherent (waves stay in phase with a fixed phase relationship), and highly directional (travels as a narrow, barely-diverging beam) — unlike ordinary light sources, which have all three properties combined.
    MCQ 8
    Q8. Joule per coulomb is the unit of:
    • A) Watt
    • B) Volt
    • C) Weber
    • D) Weber/m²
    Electric potential (voltage) is energy per unit charge, V = W/Q, measured in joules per coulomb — which is exactly the definition of the volt.
    MCQ 9
    Q9. The force on a charge moving in a magnetic field is maximum when the angle between B and v is:
    • A) 45°
    • B) 0°
    • C) 90°
    • D) 180°
    The magnetic force is F = qvB sinθ, which is maximum when sinθ = 1, i.e. θ = 90° (charge moving perpendicular to the field). At 0° or 180°, sinθ = 0 and there's no force at all.
    MCQ 10
    Q10. The light year is a unit of:
    • A) Time
    • B) Energy
    • C) Distance
    • D) Velocity
    A light year is the distance light travels in vacuum in one year (about 9.46 × 10¹² km). Despite the word "year" in its name, it measures distance, not time — a common point of confusion.
    MCQ 11
    Q11. The time period of a simple pendulum depends upon:
    • A) Mass of the bob
    • B) Length of the pendulum
    • C) Amplitude of swing
    • D) Material of the bob
    For small oscillations, T = 2π√(L/g). The period depends only on the length and local gravity — it's independent of the bob's mass and, for small angles, the amplitude.
    MCQ 12
    Q12. Which of the following is a dimensionless quantity?
    • A) Mass
    • B) Angle
    • C) Length
    • D) Time
    Angle (in radians) is arc length divided by radius — both lengths — so the units cancel, making it dimensionless. Mass, length, and time are all fundamental dimensional quantities.
    MCQ 13
    Q13. Fringe width in Young's double-slit experiment depends upon:
    • A) Slit separation
    • B) Wavelength of light
    • C) Distance between slits and screen
    • D) All of these
    Fringe width is β = λD/d, where λ is wavelength, D is slit-to-screen distance, and d is slit separation — it depends on all three.
    MCQ 14
    Q14. The SI unit of electric current is:
    • A) Coulomb
    • B) Ampere
    • C) Volt
    • D) Ohm
    The ampere is the SI base unit of current, defined as charge flow per unit time (I = Q/t). Coulomb is charge, volt is potential difference, and ohm is resistance.
    MCQ 15
    Q15. The process in which a heavy nucleus splits into smaller fragments is called:
    • A) Fission
    • B) Fusion
    • C) Chemical reaction
    • D) Endothermic reaction
    Nuclear fission is the splitting of a heavy, unstable nucleus (like Uranium-235) into lighter nuclei, releasing large amounts of energy — the process used in nuclear reactors. Fusion is the opposite: light nuclei combining into a heavier one.
    MCQ 16
    Q16. The entropy of an isolated system:
    • A) Always remains the same
    • B) Always decreases
    • C) Remains the same or increases
    • D) Always increases
    By the second law of thermodynamics, entropy of an isolated system never decreases — it stays constant for ideal reversible processes and increases for real irreversible ones. So it remains the same or increases.
    MCQ 17
    Q17. The distance between two consecutive dark and bright fringes (successive maxima/minima) is called:
    • A) Slit separation
    • B) Position of fringes
    • C) Screen distance
    • D) Fringe spacing (fringe width)
    This spacing is the fringe width, given by β = λD/d in a double-slit interference pattern.
    MCQ 18
    Q18. The length of an astronomical telescope (in normal adjustment) is:
    • A) fo + fe
    • B) fo × fe
    • C) 2fo
    • D) None of these
    In normal adjustment, the final image forms at infinity, requiring the objective's focal point to coincide with the eyepiece's focal point. So the tube length equals the sum of the two focal lengths, fo + fe.
    MCQ 19
    Q19. A body performing uniform circular motion has:
    • A) Constant velocity
    • B) Constant acceleration
    • C) Variable (centripetal) acceleration
    • D) Zero acceleration
    In uniform circular motion, speed is constant, but velocity (a vector) keeps changing direction, so there's a centripetal acceleration directed toward the center. Its magnitude is constant but its direction keeps changing, making it a variable vector quantity.
    MCQ 20
    Q20. For a planet orbiting the Sun, which quantity remains constant?
    • A) Velocity
    • B) Angular momentum
    • C) Gravitational force
    • D) Radius of orbit
    Gravity acts along the line joining planet and Sun (a central force), so it produces no torque about the Sun. By conservation of angular momentum, the planet's angular momentum stays constant even as its speed and radius vary (Kepler's second law).
    MCQ 21
    Q21. A magnetic field is NOT produced by:
    • A) A charge in motion
    • B) A charge at rest
    • C) A current-carrying conductor
    • D) None of the above
    Magnetic fields arise from moving charges (currents). A stationary charge produces only an electric field, not a magnetic one.
    MCQ 22
    Q22. Which of the following does NOT support the wave nature of light?
    • A) Diffraction
    • B) Polarization
    • C) Reflection
    • D) Compton effect
    Diffraction and polarization are classic wave phenomena, and reflection fits both wave and particle pictures. The Compton effect involves photons behaving as particles colliding with electrons — it's evidence for light's particle (quantum) nature, not its wave nature.
    MCQ 23
    Q23. The rate of change of linear momentum is defined as:
    • A) Torque
    • B) Mass
    • C) Inertia
    • D) Force
    Newton's second law states F = dp/dt — force is the rate of change of linear momentum. (Torque, by contrast, is the rate of change of angular momentum.)
    MCQ 24
    Q24. One electron volt is equal to:
    • A) 1 joule
    • B) 1 watt-second
    • C) 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ joule
    • D) None of these
    1 eV is the energy gained by one electron (charge 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C) accelerated through a 1-volt potential difference, working out to 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ joules.
    MCQ 25
    Q25. The hindrance to the flow of a fluid, offered by the fluid itself, is called:
    • A) Resistivity
    • B) Permeability
    • C) Viscosity
    • D) None of these
    Viscosity is the internal friction between adjacent fluid layers as they move past each other, resisting flow. Resistivity relates to electrical conduction; permeability relates to magnetic materials.
    MCQ 26
    Q26. A Vernier caliper can measure to an accuracy of:
    • A) 0.1 cm
    • B) 1 mm
    • C) 0.01 cm
    • D) 0.001 cm
    A standard Vernier caliper has a least count of 0.01 cm, found by dividing the main scale's smallest division by the number of Vernier scale divisions.
    MCQ 27
    Q27. The maximum height (H) reached in projectile motion is given by:
    • A) H = (V₀² sin²θ)/2g
    • B) H = (V₀² sin2θ)/2g
    • C) H = (V₀² cos²θ)/g
    • D) H = V₀²/2g
    The vertical component of initial velocity is V₀sinθ. Using v² = u² − 2gH with final vertical velocity zero at the peak, H = (V₀sinθ)²/2g = (V₀²sin²θ)/2g.
    MCQ 28
    Q28. A choke coil has:
    • A) High resistance and high inductance
    • B) Low resistance and high inductance
    • C) Low resistance and low inductance
    • D) None of these
    A choke coil limits AC current using inductive reactance while wasting minimal power as heat. It's built with thick, low-resistance wire wound on an iron core to give high inductance with low resistance.
    MCQ 29
    Q29. The distance between two consecutive nodes (or two consecutive antinodes) in a stationary wave is:
    • A) λ/2
    • B) λ
    • C) λ/4
    • D) 2λ
    In a standing wave, nodes and antinodes alternate every λ/4, but two consecutive nodes (or two consecutive antinodes) of the same type are separated by half a wavelength, λ/2.
    MCQ 30
    Q30. Artificial gravity in a spaceship can be produced by:
    • A) Translatory motion
    • B) Vibratory motion
    • C) Orbital motion
    • D) Spin (rotational) motion
    Spinning a spaceship about its axis pushes objects toward the outer wall via the centripetal effect, mimicking the sensation of gravity — the basis for artificial-gravity designs like a rotating space station.
    MCQ 31
    Q31. We distinguish musical sounds of the same pitch and loudness (e.g. same note from different instruments) by their:
    • A) Amplitude
    • B) Frequency
    • C) Quality (timbre)
    • D) Pitch
    Quality (timbre) lets us tell apart two sounds of the same pitch and loudness, arising from differences in the harmonic/overtone content each instrument produces.
    MCQ 32
    Q32. The magnetic field inside a toroid is given by:
    • A) B = μ₀nI
    • B) B = μ₀nI/2πr
    • C) B = μ₀nI/2
    • D) B = μ₀nI/2a
    Applying Ampère's law around a circular path of radius r inside a toroid gives B = μ₀NI/(2πr), where N is the total number of turns and I is the current — matching this option.
    MCQ 33
    Q33. If the p-type side (anode) of a diode is connected to the positive terminal and the n-type side (cathode) to the negative terminal of a battery, the diode is:
    • A) Forward biased
    • B) Reverse biased
    • C) Both
    • D) None
    Connecting p-type to positive and n-type to negative narrows the depletion region and lets current flow easily — this connection is, by definition, forward bias.
    MCQ 34
    Q34. A generator converts:
    • A) Electrical energy into heat energy
    • B) Mechanical energy into electrical energy
    • C) Electrical energy into light energy
    • D) Electrical energy into mechanical energy
    A generator (dynamo) works by electromagnetic induction, converting mechanical energy (rotation of a coil or magnet) into electrical energy. A motor does the reverse.
    MCQ 35
    Q35. The first law of thermodynamics represents the conservation of:
    • A) Energy
    • B) Mass
    • C) Charge
    • D) Momentum
    The first law (ΔU = Q − W) is the law of conservation of energy applied to thermodynamic systems — energy transfers as heat or work but is never created or destroyed.
    MCQ 36
    Q36. The amount of heat required to raise the temperature of 1 kg of a substance by 1°C is called its:
    • A) Heat capacity
    • B) Molar heat capacity
    • C) Latent heat
    • D) Specific heat capacity
    Specific heat capacity is defined per unit mass (1 kg) per degree rise. Heat capacity (without "specific") refers to a whole object's total mass, molar heat capacity is per mole, and latent heat relates to phase changes at constant temperature.
    MCQ 37
    Q37. If the mass of the bob of a simple pendulum is doubled, its time period will:
    • A) Remain the same
    • B) Become twice
    • C) Become half
    • D) Become four times
    T = 2π√(L/g) has no dependence on mass at all — only length and gravity matter. Doubling the mass changes nothing about the period.
    MCQ 38
    Q38. The coefficient of static friction is generally:
    • A) Greater than the coefficient of kinetic friction
    • B) Smaller than the coefficient of kinetic friction
    • C) Equal to the coefficient of kinetic friction
    • D) The reciprocal of the coefficient of kinetic friction
    It typically takes more force to start an object moving from rest than to keep it sliding once moving, which is why the coefficient of static friction (μs) is usually slightly greater than kinetic friction (μk).
    MCQ 39
    Q39. According to Bragg's law, the condition for diffraction of X-rays from a crystal is:
    • A) d sinθ = mπ
    • B) d sinθ = (m + ½)π
    • C) 2d sinθ = mλ
    • D) None of these
    Bragg's law states that constructive interference of X-rays reflected from successive crystal planes occurs when 2d sinθ = mλ, where d is the spacing between planes, θ is the glancing angle, m is an integer order, and λ is the X-ray wavelength.
    MCQ 40
    Q40. Which of the following is a vector quantity?
    • A) Electric flux
    • B) Magnetic flux
    • C) EMF
    • D) Electric field intensity
    Electric flux, magnetic flux, and EMF are all scalar quantities (magnitude only). Electric field intensity (E) has both magnitude and direction at every point in space, making it a vector.
    MCQ 41
    Q41. The phase angle (φ) between voltage and current in a series AC circuit containing resistance R and inductance L is given by:
    • A) φ = tan⁻¹(X_L/R)
    • B) φ = tan(X_L/R)
    • C) φ = tan⁻¹(R/X_L)
    • D) φ = tan⁻¹[(X_L−X_C)/R]
    In a series R-L circuit, current lags voltage by φ = tan⁻¹(X_L/R), where X_L = ωL is the inductive reactance — this follows from the impedance triangle where R is the base and X_L is the perpendicular side. (Option D applies to a full R-L-C circuit, not this one.)
